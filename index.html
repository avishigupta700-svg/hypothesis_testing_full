📖 Quick Topic Summary

Z-Test

Used to test whether a sample mean differs from a population mean when the population standard deviation (σ) is known and the sample size is large (n ≥ 30). Assumes data is normally distributed.

z = (x̄ − μ₀) / (σ / √n)
Two-prop: z = (p̂₁ − p̂₂) / √[p̂(1−p̂)(1/n₁+1/n₂)]

T-Test

Used when σ is unknown and/or the sample is small (n < 30). Three forms: one-sample (compare to μ), independent two-sample (compare two groups), and paired (compare matched pairs). Uses t-distribution with df = n−1.

t = (x̄ − μ₀) / (s / √n) [one-sample]

ANOVA

Analysis of Variance tests equality of means across three or more groups simultaneously. Uses the F-statistic (ratio of between-group to within-group variance). Assumes normality, equal variances (Levene's), and independence.

F = MS_between / MS_within = (SS_B/df_B) / (SS_W/df_W)

Chi-Square (χ²)

Non-parametric test for categorical data. Two uses: Goodness-of-Fit (does observed distribution match expected?) and Test of Independence (are two categorical variables related?). df = (r−1)(c−1) for contingency tables.

χ² = Σ [ (O − E)² / E ]

Key Concepts

Null Hypothesis (H₀): No effect/difference. p-value: Probability of observing results at least as extreme, assuming H₀ is true. Reject H₀ when p < α. Type I error (α): Rejecting a true H₀. Type II error (β): Failing to reject a false H₀.

Power = 1 − β | Common α = 0.05

Test Selection Guide

σ known, large n → Z-test
σ unknown / small n → T-test
3+ group means → ANOVA
Categorical frequencies → Chi-Square
Non-normal / ordinal → Non-parametric alternatives

🟢 Easy — Questions 1–10
1
What is the null hypothesis (H₀) in hypothesis testing?
Fundamentals
H₀ represents the "default" position — no effect or no difference. We test evidence against it, and reject it only when data provide sufficiently strong evidence (p < α).
2
A z-test is most appropriate when:
Z-Test
A z-test requires knowledge of σ (population SD) and benefits from large samples where the Central Limit Theorem ensures a normal sampling distribution.
3
What does the p-value represent in hypothesis testing?
Fundamentals
The p-value does NOT tell us the probability H₀ is true. It tells us: if H₀ were true, how likely is our observed result (or more extreme)? A small p-value means the data are unlikely under H₀.
4
A Type I error occurs when:
Error Types
Type I error (false positive) = rejecting H₀ when it's true. Its probability is α (the significance level). Type II error (false negative) = failing to reject a false H₀, with probability β.
5
In a one-sample t-test with n = 15 observations, the degrees of freedom (df) equals:
T-Test
For a one-sample t-test: df = n − 1 = 15 − 1 = 14. One degree of freedom is "used up" to estimate the sample mean (x̄) before we can estimate variability.
6
ANOVA is used to compare:
ANOVA
ANOVA (Analysis of Variance) compares means across 3+ groups in one test, avoiding the inflated Type I error that would result from running multiple t-tests.
7
Which test is most appropriate for testing whether a categorical variable is independent of another categorical variable?
Chi-Square
The chi-square test of independence determines whether two categorical variables are associated. For example: is political affiliation independent of gender? It uses a contingency table of observed vs expected frequencies.
8
The critical value for a two-tailed z-test at significance level α = 0.05 is:
Z-Test
For α = 0.05 two-tailed, we split α into 0.025 per tail. z₀.₀₂₅ = 1.96. For α = 0.01 two-tailed: ±2.576. For α = 0.10 two-tailed: ±1.645. These are essential critical values to memorise.
9
What is the chi-square statistic formula?
Chi-Square
χ² = Σ[(O − E)² / E], summed over all cells. O = observed frequency, E = expected frequency. Squaring the difference ensures direction doesn't matter, and dividing by E gives a proportional measure of discrepancy.
10
When should a paired t-test be used instead of an independent samples t-test?
T-Test
Paired t-tests are used for matched/related data — e.g., before-and-after measurements on the same subject, or matched controls. Pairing reduces variability and increases statistical power by controlling for individual differences.
🟡 Medium — Questions 11–20
11
In a one-way ANOVA with k = 4 groups and a total of N = 40 observations, what are the between-group and within-group degrees of freedom?
ANOVA
df_between = k − 1 = 4 − 1 = 3; df_within = N − k = 40 − 4 = 36. Total df = N − 1 = 39, which checks out (3 + 36 = 39). These df values are used to look up the critical F-value.
12
Which assumption is NOT required for a one-way ANOVA?
ANOVA
ANOVA requires a continuous (interval/ratio) dependent variable, not a categorical one. Chi-square tests handle categorical outcomes. The three core ANOVA assumptions are: independence, normality, and homogeneity of variance.
13
A researcher runs a z-test and gets z = 2.10. Using α = 0.05 (two-tailed), what is the conclusion?
Z-Test
|z| = 2.10 > 1.96 (the critical value at α = 0.05, two-tailed), so we reject H₀. Note: we never "accept" H₀; we either reject it or fail to reject it. The p-value here ≈ 0.036 < 0.05.
14
For a chi-square test of independence with a 3×4 contingency table, the degrees of freedom are:
Chi-Square
For a contingency table: df = (rows − 1) × (columns − 1) = (3 − 1) × (4 − 1) = 2 × 3 = 6. Each marginal total loses one degree of freedom.
15
The F-statistic in ANOVA is calculated as:
ANOVA
F = MS_between / MS_within. A large F means between-group variability (due to the factor) is much larger than within-group variability (random error), suggesting the group means differ significantly. F is always ≥ 0.
16
A researcher wants to compare blood pressure readings for the same patients before and after a medication. The most appropriate test is:
T-Test
Same subjects measured twice = matched/dependent data → paired t-test. It computes the difference (d = after − before) for each patient and tests H₀: μ_d = 0. This design controls for individual variation and is more powerful than an independent t-test.
17
The expected frequency for a cell in a chi-square test of independence is calculated as:
Chi-Square
E_ij = (Row_i total × Column_j total) / N. This assumes independence — if the two variables were independent, the joint probability would be the product of the marginal probabilities. At least 80% of cells should have E ≥ 5 for validity.
18
Which of the following correctly describes the relationship between α (significance level) and the power of a test?
Fundamentals
Raising α widens the rejection region → more likely to reject H₀ (both correctly and incorrectly) → higher power (1 − β) but more Type I errors. This classic trade-off is why α is set conservatively (e.g., 0.05). Power also depends on effect size and sample size.
19
An independent samples t-test assumes which of the following about the two populations?
T-Test
The standard (pooled) independent t-test assumes σ₁² ≈ σ₂² (Levene's test checks this). If variances differ significantly, use Welch's t-test, which adjusts the degrees of freedom using the Welch–Satterthwaite equation. Equal sample sizes are not required.
20
A chi-square goodness-of-fit test with 5 categories will have how many degrees of freedom?
Chi-Square
For a goodness-of-fit test: df = k − 1 = 5 − 1 = 4, where k = number of categories. One degree of freedom is lost because all expected frequencies must sum to n (a constraint). If parameters are estimated from data, additional df are lost.
🔴 Hard — Questions 21–30
21
When ANOVA yields a significant F-test, a researcher conducts Tukey's HSD post-hoc test. Why is post-hoc testing necessary?
ANOVA
A significant F only tells us at least one mean differs — not which ones. Post-hoc tests (Tukey's HSD, Bonferroni, Scheffé, Dunnett's) make all pairwise comparisons while controlling FWER. Tukey's HSD is preferred when all pairwise comparisons are of equal interest and group sizes are equal.
22
Welch's t-test differs from the standard independent samples t-test primarily in that it:
T-Test
Welch's t-test relaxes the homogeneity of variance assumption by using separate variance estimates for each group and applying the Welch–Satterthwaite approximation to compute adjusted (non-integer) df. It is generally recommended as the default over the pooled t-test.
23
Cohen's d for effect size in a two-sample t-test is calculated as:
T-Test
Cohen's d = (x̄₁ − x̄₂) / s_pooled, where s_pooled = √[(s₁²(n₁−1) + s₂²(n₂−1)) / (n₁+n₂−2)]. Benchmarks: d = 0.2 (small), 0.5 (medium), 0.8 (large). Statistical significance ≠ practical significance; large n can make tiny effects significant.
24
In a two-way ANOVA, what does a significant interaction effect indicate?
ANOVA
An interaction (A×B) means the effect of factor A depends on the level of factor B — the lines in an interaction plot are non-parallel. When interactions are present, main effects must be interpreted cautiously, as simple effects may be more meaningful.
25
Yates' continuity correction is applied to the chi-square test when:
Chi-Square
Yates' correction subtracts 0.5 from |O − E| before squaring — χ² = Σ(|O − E| − 0.5)² / E — to reduce the overestimation of chi-square in 2×2 tables with small expected counts. However, Fisher's Exact Test is often preferred for 2×2 tables when any E < 5.
26
What is the non-parametric equivalent of the one-way ANOVA when assumptions of normality are violated?
ANOVA
The Kruskal–Wallis test is the rank-based non-parametric alternative to one-way ANOVA, comparing 3+ independent groups. Mann–Whitney U: two independent groups. Wilcoxon signed-rank: paired data. Friedman: repeated measures (non-parametric two-way ANOVA).
27
In hypothesis testing, increasing the sample size (n) while keeping everything else constant will:
Fundamentals
SE = σ/√n, so increasing n decreases SE → smaller confidence intervals → larger test statistics for the same effect → higher power. However, very large samples can make trivially small, practically meaningless differences statistically significant — always consider effect size alongside p-values.
28
The eta-squared (η²) statistic in ANOVA represents:
ANOVA
η² = SS_between / SS_total, ranging 0 to 1. Benchmarks (Cohen): η² = 0.01 (small), 0.06 (medium), 0.14 (large). Partial η² (η²_p) is preferred in multi-factor ANOVA; omega-squared (ω²) is less biased than η² and better for generalization.
29
A z-test for the difference between two population proportions uses the pooled proportion (p̂) under H₀. The correct formula for p̂ is:
Z-Test
Under H₀: p₁ = p₂, we pool: p̂ = (x₁ + x₂)/(n₁ + n₂), where x₁, x₂ are the counts of successes. This uses all available data to get the best single estimate of the common proportion. The SE is then √[p̂(1−p̂)(1/n₁+1/n₂)].
30
A study applies one-way ANOVA and finds F(3, 56) = 4.82, p = 0.004. Tukey's HSD post-hoc at α = 0.05 shows only Group 1 vs. Group 3 is significant. Which of the following is the most correct interpretation?
ANOVA
F(3, 56) = 4.82, p = 0.004 → overall significance is real. Post-hoc narrows it: the Group 1 vs. Group 3 difference drives this result. Not all pairs need to be significant — Tukey's HSD controls FWER at 0.05 across all 6 pairwise comparisons. This is textbook ANOVA + post-hoc interpretation.
